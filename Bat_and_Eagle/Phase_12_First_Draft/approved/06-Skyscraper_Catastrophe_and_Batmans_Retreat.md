## 06-Skyscraper_Catastrophe_and_Batmans_Retreat

The rain hammered against the cave, a constant, drumming reminder of the city’s endless misery. Inside, the chill was absolute. It seeped from the stone, from the steel platforms, from the rows of dead-eyed monitors.

“He’s hit the Kajiwara building,” Oracle’s voice, sharp and clean, cut through the gloom. No static. No emotion. Just data.

On the main screen, a figure in white moved through the rain-lashed penthouse suite. A ghost against the storm. The helmet was sculpted, avian, with a sharp, beak-like visor. A short, feathered cape snapped behind him as he moved. It was all wrong. Too bright. Too clean for this city.

Batman watched, his jaw a knot of granite beneath the cowl. He was already in the cockpit of the Tumbler, systems humming to life around him. His objective was clear. Intercept. Apprehend.

The figure on the screen didn’t brawl. He executed. He flowed through rooms filled with panicked men in cheap suits, their pistols barking uselessly. His movements were a blur, impossibly fast. He ran along a glass wall, defying gravity for three heartbeats, before launching himself into their midst. A flash of motion, and razor-edged boomerangs, shaped like the wings of a predator bird, sliced through the air. They returned to a gloved hand, dripping. Men fell. They didn’t get back up.

“He’s not containing them,” Batman growled, his voice a low vibration in the armored cockpit. “He’s purging them.”

“Analysis confirms lethal force,” Oracle replied. “Every strike is a kill shot. He’s moving down, floor by floor. Systematically.”

The engine roared, a caged beast waking from a shallow sleep. The cave exit ground open. He had to get there. He had to stop this.

Then, Oracle’s voice tightened. A subtle shift. The sound of a professional seeing a variable she couldn’t account for. “Wait. Hold.”

The main screen froze, then rewound. The white vigilante smashed through a plate-glass window on the 48th floor, rappelling down the slick face of the building into the raging storm. He was leaving. His assault was over.

“He’s broken off contact,” Batman said.

“He has,” Oracle confirmed. “But watch office 48-C. His point of egress.”

She highlighted a corner office. Empty. A flicker in the corner. A small, blinking red light on a device taped to a support column. A device the white-clad killer had run right past. It wasn’t his.

“It’s a secondary…”

The world on the screen dissolved into white light. The audio feed shrieked with static and then died. The external seismographs in the cave jumped, the needles scratching frantically. A low, gut-deep rumble vibrated through the cockpit floor, a ghost of the distant blast.

Every news feed lit up at once. Helicopters circled the pillar of smoke that had been a skyscraper. The top twenty floors were gone. Sheared off. A cascade of steel, glass, and fire plunged into the streets below. The rain couldn't quench it.

“Catastrophic structural failure,” Oracle’s voice was a whisper now. “God… the casualties…”

The GCPD spokesperson was on screen before the first body was pulled from the rubble. A fat man with a thin mustache and panicked eyes, reading from a script. He used the words ‘vigilante terrorism.’ He said ‘Batman.’ He said the city would no longer tolerate these masked freaks waging their wars on our streets. The narrative was set. The lie was already halfway around the world while the truth was still trying to pull its boots on.

He could see it all laid out. The riots, starting in the panicked downtown streets and spreading like a virus. The fear, weaponized by the corrupt and the craven. The city turning on itself. And on him.

He could go out there. Wade into the fire. Try to save who he could. He’d be a target. For the cops. For every two-bit hood who saw a chance to make a name for himself. For the public who now saw a monster. His presence would be gasoline on the flames. He would be captured, or killed, or worse—a symbol of the very chaos he fought to contain.

Or he could vanish. Let them have their monster. Let the city show its true face. A best bad choice. The only choice.

The engine noise died with a final cough. He killed the lights. The monitors. Everything.

“Go dark, Oracle,” he commanded. His own voice sounded alien in the sudden, total silence. “All channels. Now.”

He stepped out of the Tumbler into the oppressive black of the cave. The distant sirens were the only sound left, a mournful dirge for a city already devouring itself. He stood alone, the weight of the falling skyscraper, of all those lives, pressing down until he thought his bones would break. A failure. He had failed them all.

---

Miles away, in a sterile, windowless room, Ken Washio stripped off a blood-stained glove. His white uniform was torn, smeared with soot. A screen on the wall flickered to life, displaying a single line of coded text.

`COLLATERAL UNACCEPTABLE. OBJECTIVE REMAINS. PROCEED.`

His jaw tightened. He had not planted the bomb. The mission was compromised. But the orders were clear.

---

In a new hole, buried deep in the city’s electronic ghost, a single monitor glowed. Barbara Gordon watched the city burn through a dozen encrypted feeds. Then, a notification blinked in the corner of her screen. An anonymous data drop. A single, heavily encrypted file recovered from a hardened server in the skyscraper’s wreckage.

Her fingers flew across the keyboard. Code scrolled past, locks breaking one by one. She leaned closer, her face lit by the cold, green text. Her work had just begun.
